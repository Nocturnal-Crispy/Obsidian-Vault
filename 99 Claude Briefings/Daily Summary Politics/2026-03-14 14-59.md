## Policy & Regulation

### Data Center Regulation Sparks Rare Bipartisan Action in Statehouses
State lawmakers across the country have proposed more than 300 bills to regulate data centers in 2026, making this a rare bipartisan issue. Some legislators are pushing moratoriums on data center construction, citing increased utility costs for surrounding communities. The issue played a major role in 2025 gubernatorial races in Virginia and New Jersey, and with 36 governor's elections in 2026, it is expected to remain politically salient through November.

**What changed:** The volume of bills (300+) signals this has moved from a niche concern to a mainstream political issue.
**Who is affected:** Cloud providers, datacenter operators (AWS, Google, Microsoft, Meta), utility companies, local communities near datacenter clusters.
**What to watch:** Whether any moratoriums pass; how the tech industry lobbying response shapes up; whether federal preemption is attempted.
**Trend:** Escalating Trend

### Federal vs. State AI Regulation — The Preemption Battle Intensifies
The central question in AI regulation has shifted from "preempt something with nothing" to "preempt something with something" — meaning the debate is now about whether federal AI rules should replace the growing patchwork of state laws with a concrete framework. GOP lawmakers' attempt to include a 10-year moratorium on state AI laws in Trump's tax bill was stripped by the Senate. Trump has since signed an executive order creating a task force to challenge state AI measures in court.

**What changed:** The executive order task force represents a new escalation — the federal government is now actively litigating against state AI regulations.
**Who is affected:** AI companies operating across multiple states, state legislators, consumers protected by state-level AI laws.
**What to watch:** Court challenges to state AI laws; whether a federal AI framework bill gains traction before midterms.
**Trend:** Escalating Trend

### New York AI Law Takes Effect in March
New York's AI law became effective this month, adding to the growing patchwork of state-level AI regulation. Delayed enforcement provisions of the federal Take It Down Act follow in May.

**What changed:** Another major state AI law is now enforceable.
**Who is affected:** AI companies operating in or serving New York markets; content platforms (Take It Down Act).
**What to watch:** Enforcement actions; compliance burden on smaller AI companies.
**Trend:** Continuing Trend

With November midterm elections threatening their majority, Congressional Republicans face growing pressure to deliver on AI regulation, online safety, and broader tech policy. 97% of the American public supports some form of AI regulation, adding urgency. The window to pass legislation while holding a trifecta is closing.

**What changed:** The midterm timeline is creating legislative urgency that didn't exist six months ago.
**Who is affected:** Tech companies preparing for potential federal regulation; voters evaluating candidates on tech policy.
**What to watch:** Whether any AI or online safety bills advance through committee before the August recess.
**Trend:** Continuing Trend

### Trump Administration Feuds with Allies Over Tech Regulation
The administration has repeatedly criticized key U.S. allies over regulation of American tech companies. The EU's Digital Services Act and Digital Markets Act have been used to levy large fines against Apple, Meta, X, and other U.S. companies, drawing sharp responses from the White House.

**What changed:** The diplomatic tension over tech regulation is becoming a recurring friction point in transatlantic relations.
**Who is affected:** U.S. tech companies operating in the EU; transatlantic trade relations.
**What to watch:** Whether tariff or trade retaliation is linked to tech regulation disputes; EU enforcement actions in Q2.
**Trend:** Continuing Trend

### Nintendo Sues U.S. Government Over Console Tariffs
Nintendo filed a lawsuit on March 6 against the U.S. government over tariffs on the Switch 2, which caused delayed pre-orders and price increases. This is the first major legal challenge to consumer electronics tariffs by a gaming company.

**What changed:** A major multinational is directly challenging U.S. trade policy in court over gaming hardware.
**Who is affected:** Console makers, hardware importers, consumers facing higher prices.
**What to watch:** Whether other hardware companies (Sony, Valve) join or file similar suits; court timeline.
**Trend:** New Trend
