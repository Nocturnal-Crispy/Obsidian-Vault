## Story 1: Iran War Day 29 — Houthis Enter War, Israel Strikes Nuclear Sites, Conflict Widens
**Heat:** Critical | **Momentum:** ESCALATED | **Source Tier:** 1 (Confirmed)
**Categories:** Politics, Security (cross-linked to [[99 Claude Briefings/Daily Summary Tech/2026-03-28]])

The Iran conflict escalated dramatically on multiple fronts today:

**Houthi entry:** Yemen's Houthi rebels fired a barrage of ballistic missiles at "sensitive Israeli military sites" in southern Israel, marking their formal entry into the war. Israel intercepted one missile; no casualties reported. A senior Houthi official said the group is considering closing the Bab al-Mandeb Strait, through which roughly one-eighth of global trade passes. Combined with the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed for a month, this threatens two of the world's most critical shipping chokepoints simultaneously.

**Nuclear strikes:** Over 50 Israeli Air Force jets struck Iran's Khandab heavy water reactor in Arak, a uranium enrichment facility in Ardakan, and the Bushehr nuclear plant. Iran's Atomic Energy Organization reported no casualties or technical disruptions at Bushehr. Two major steel plants (Khuzestan Steel, Mobarakeh Steel) were also hit.

**Iranian retaliation threats:** Foreign Minister Araghchi warned Iran will "exact a heavy price," specifically threatening to attack six steel plants in Israel and Gulf states (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain). Deaths from the conflict have now been reported across the UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, West Bank, Oman, Bahrain, and Saudi Arabia.

**Diplomatic track:** Trump's extended energy-strike pause runs to April 6. Pakistan continues relaying messages between US and Iranian officials.

**Why it matters:** The Houthi entry is the most significant escalation this week. If Houthis close the Bab al-Mandeb Strait while the Strait of Hormuz remains disrupted, global shipping faces a dual-chokepoint crisis not seen in modern history. Iran's threat to attack Gulf state industrial sites raises the prospect of the war spreading beyond the US-Israel-Iran triangle into a regional conflict affecting global energy and manufacturing supply chains. The April 6 energy-strike deadline remains the next critical inflection point, but events are outpacing the diplomatic timeline.

## Story 2: DHS Shutdown Stalemate — House Rejects Senate Deal, Passes Own Bill, Deadlock Continues
**Heat:** Critical | **Momentum:** SUSTAINED | **Source Tier:** 1 (Confirmed)

Yesterday's apparent breakthrough collapsed. The sequence:

1. **Senate deal (early Friday):** Unanimous vote to fund most of DHS (TSA, Coast Guard, agencies) through September 30, excluding ICE and CBP.
2. **House rejection:** Speaker Johnson called the Senate bill a "joke" and refused to bring it to a vote.
3. **House counter-bill:** Passed 213-203 (three Democrats crossing over: Davis, Gluesenkamp Perez, Cuellar) — a 60-day stopgap funding all of DHS including ICE and CBP through May 22.
4. **Senate response:** Democrats declared the House bill "dead on arrival."

Trump signed an executive order directing DHS to "immediately pay" TSA agents using reconciliation bill funds and declared an "airport emergency." The legal viability of this funding mechanism is unclear.

The shutdown is now in its 43rd day. TSA damage: 480+ officers quit, 40-50% callout rates at some airports, wait times exceeding 4.5 hours.

**Why it matters:** The DHS shutdown has entered a new phase — both chambers have passed bills, but neither will accept the other's. The core disagreement (ICE/CBP funding) is ideological and unlikely to resolve quickly. Trump's executive order may provide temporary relief for TSA workers but doesn't address the structural impasse. The workforce damage is permanent — officers who quit during a 43-day pay gap won't return, and TSA's training pipeline takes months. This is now the longest partial government shutdown in US history.
