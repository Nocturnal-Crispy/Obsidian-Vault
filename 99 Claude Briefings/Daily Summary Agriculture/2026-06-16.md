# Agriculture Intelligence — 2026-06-16

## Livestock

### 1. Cattle Prices Up 17.7% YoY — Herd Contraction Continues
**Heat: High | Momentum: SUSTAINED | Source: Tier 1 (Confirmed)**

Farm-level cattle prices increased 4.8% from March to April 2026 and are 17.7% higher YoY. USDA projects an 11.2% increase for full-year 2026 (prediction interval: 2.0–22.0%). The cyclical herd contraction continues to drive tight supplies. August live cattle futures were at $239.70 (+$2.97) and feeder cattle at $354.10 (+$3.45) as of June 9.

**Why it matters:** The cattle bull market shows no signs of reversing. Producers benefit from record prices but feed cost margins remain compressed. Retail beef prices continue to rise for consumers.

### 2. HPAI Avian Flu Now Confirmed in Dairy Cattle Across 19 States
**Heat: High | Momentum: SUSTAINED | Source: Tier 1 (Confirmed)**

H5N1 HPAI has been confirmed in dairy cattle in 19 states (AZ, CA, CO, ID, IA, KS, MI, MN, NE, NV, NM, NC, OH, OK, SD, TX, UT, WI, WY). Unlike poultry, dairy cows are not culled after infection but face short-term milk production losses. CDC maintains public health risk is low. Poultry losses continue to pressure egg supply and pricing — an estimated 184M+ laying hens affected across all 50 states.

**Why it matters:** Geographic spread continues expanding. The 19-state dairy impact adds a novel dimension to HPAI management. Egg price volatility remains high. No resolution timeline in sight.

## Crops

### 3. Winter Wheat Crop Smallest Since 1965 — Record-Low Acres
**Heat: High | Momentum: NEW | Source: Tier 1 (Confirmed)**

USDA estimated the 2026-27 winter wheat crop at 1.03 billion bushels, down 18M from its initial May forecast and nearly 27% below last year — the smallest since 1965. Total wheat plantings at 43.775M acres (down from 45.328M in 2025) would be the lowest since records began in 1919.

**Why it matters:** Record-low wheat plantings have major implications for US food security and export capacity. Wheat futures should find support, though short-term weakness from corn/crude oil pressure may persist. Global buyers may shift sourcing.

### 4. Corn Acreage Down 3%, Soybean Acreage Up on Profitability
**Heat: Medium | Momentum: SUSTAINED | Source: Tier 1 (Confirmed)**

US corn planting intentions at 95.338M acres, down 3% (3.45M acres) from 2025. Projected yield: 183 bu/acre for a 15.8B bushel crop (down ~7% from 2025). Soybean acreage is rising on stronger profitability and crop rotation, with yields expected at 53.0 bu/acre and production up 188M bushels to 4.45B bushels.

**Why it matters:** The corn-to-soybean rotation shift is the dominant planting trend for 2026. Lower corn production tightens feed supply — watch for interaction with cattle price dynamics. June 30 Acreage and Grain Stocks reports will be the next major market-moving events.

## Gardening (Zone 6a/6b — Hermitage, PA)

### 5. Mid-June Update: Late Blight and Downy Mildew Active in PA
**Heat: Medium | Momentum: NEW | Source: Tier 2 (Reputable)**

**Current pest alerts for PA:**
- **Late blight (Phytophthora infestans):** Now reported active in Pennsylvania for the 2026 season. #1 tomato threat in humid summers. Can destroy a planting in days. Scout daily.
- **Cucurbit downy mildew:** Also reported in PA for 2026. Affects cucumbers, squash, melons. Watch for yellow angular leaf spots.
- **Tomato hornworms:** Peak season beginning. Look for dark green droppings as early detection.
- **Japanese beetles:** Arriving late June. Hand-pick or trap.

**What to do this week:**
- Final warm-season transplants should go in immediately
- Succession-sow bush beans and summer squash every 2 weeks
- Deep water and heavy mulch — heat stress period begins
- Scout tomatoes and cucurbits daily for disease in humid weather
- If using insect control, identify beneficial insects first (Penn State Extension IPM hotline)

### 6. Section 232 Tariff Reduction on Agricultural Equipment
**Heat: Medium | Momentum: NEW | Source: Tier 1 (Confirmed)**

Effective June 8, Section 232 tariffs on ag equipment (combines, harvesters, tractors, plows, mowers) reduced from 25% to 15%. Products with ≥85% US-smelted metal qualify for 10%. Runs through December 31, 2027.

**Why it matters:** Direct cost relief for equipment purchases and replacement cycles. Farmers buying or leasing new equipment in the next 18 months benefit from reduced import costs on metal-intensive machinery.
