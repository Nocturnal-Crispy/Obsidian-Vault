## Livestock

### 1. Record Drought: 79% of Beef Cattle in Drought Areas — Herd Rebuild Effectively Stalled
**Heat:** Critical | **Momentum:** SUSTAINED | **Source:** Tier 1 (USDA, Drought.gov, DTN Progressive Farmer, UCS)

The drought situation for US livestock remains at crisis levels with no improvement:

- 79% of beef cow herd in drought-affected areas (26 states, 88% of production)
- 63% of all cattle in moderate or more intense drought
- 51% of alfalfa hay in drought — feed availability tightening
- DSCI at 202 — highest end-of-April value in Drought Monitor history
- Ranchers in "waiting game" mode — forced liquidation decisions approaching if pasture conditions don't improve by July
- USDA has paid out $18B in disaster assistance under the American Relief Act; application deadline extended to August
- USDA WASDE-671 projects lower beef production through 2027

Cash trade picked up Friday at $255–258 nationally. Feeder cattle index at ~$367.26, down $4.23. Feed costs and drought squeezing margins.

**Strategic Impact:** The herd rebuild begun in late 2024 is effectively stalled. Forced liquidation would create a short-term supply surge (temporary price drop) followed by structural shortage (significantly higher prices 2027–2028). This remains the most significant long-term US beef supply risk in a decade. The $18B disaster payout and August deadline extension signal USDA expects worsening conditions through summer.

### 2. HPAI Surveillance Remains Elevated — No New Escalation
**Heat:** High | **Momentum:** SUSTAINED | **Source:** Tier 1 (APHIS, CDC)

H5N1 HPAI continues across US poultry and dairy cattle (18+ states). No new escalation events since Friday. CDC maintains "low" public health risk. Arctic mammalian detections (polar bears, walruses, arctic foxes) continue to signal viral adaptation but no new human clusters.

**Strategic Impact:** Status quo — elevated but not escalating. Biosecurity protocols remain critical. Monitor for any changes in CDC risk assessment.

## Crops

### 3. Winter Wheat Crisis: Lowest Acreage Since 1919, Production Lowest Since 1965/66
**Heat:** Critical | **Momentum:** SUSTAINED | **Source:** Tier 1 (USDA, USDA ERS, Farm Progress, ABC News)

The winter wheat disaster continues with no relief in sight:

- USDA forecasts 2026 wheat acreage as the **lowest since 1919**
- Winter wheat production projected at 1,048M bushels — **lowest since 1965/66**, down 25% YoY
- Hard Red Winter production forecast at 515M bushels — **down 36% YoY**
- 32% national abandonment rate
- Nebraska: 82% rated very poor to poor
- Kansas: 93% headed, 58% poor to very poor
- Oklahoma: 48% poor to very poor
- 70% of winter wheat in drought areas; 98% of cotton in drought
- Drought covers 62%+ of Lower 48 — worst spring drought on record
- DSCI above 200 continuously since late April

**Strategic Impact:** This is approaching food security significance. US wheat exports will decline materially, supporting higher bread/food prices domestically. Global wheat supply is also tight. The USDA ERS Wheat Outlook (May 2026) confirms structural tightening across all wheat classes. If drought persists through June–July, corn and soybean yields are also at risk.

### 4. Corn and Soybean Planting Progress Good, but Drought Risk Mounting
**Heat:** Medium | **Momentum:** SUSTAINED | **Source:** Tier 1 (USDA NASS, RFD-TV)

Corn and soybean planting remains ahead of the five-year average. However:

- Only 27% of corn and 30% of soybean production areas currently in drought — Midwest relatively spared so far
- The critical window is late June–July: if drought expands into the Corn Belt during pollination, yield losses could be severe
- Markets not yet pricing in severe corn yield failure — a hot, dry June would trigger rapid futures repricing

**Strategic Impact:** The gap between planted acreage and probable harvested acreage is growing. Corn pollination is the make-or-break event. Monitor weekly Drought Monitor expansions into IL, IN, IA, OH.

## Gardening

### 5. Zone 6a/6b (Hermitage, PA): June Transition — Full Growing Season Begins
**Heat:** Low | **Momentum:** SEASONAL | **Source:** Tier 1/2 (Penn State Extension)

June 1 guidance for Zone 6:

- **Planting:** All warm-season crops should be established. Succession sow beans, lettuce, radish every 2 weeks. Direct sow squash, melons, cucumbers if not already done.
- **Maintenance:** Deep water 1–1.5"/week minimum given drought. Mulch all beds 2–3" to conserve moisture. Side-dress tomatoes and corn with nitrogen as they size up.
- **Pest watch (active now):**
  - Spotted lanternfly nymphs increasingly active — check tree of heaven, grapes, hops
  - Squash vine borer adults have emerged — apply Bt or row cover before egg-laying
  - Japanese beetles approaching — handpick or neem at first sign
  - Monitor for tomato hornworm as plants size up
  - Cucumber beetles active — watch for bacterial wilt transmission
- **Harvest:** Strawberries, lettuce, radish, peas, early greens, garlic scapes
- **Planning:** Start fall brassica seeds indoors (late June transplant target)

**Strategic Impact:** Regional drought conditions demand extra irrigation attention. Municipal water restrictions possible if drought persists — rain barrel and drip irrigation systems are a wise investment now. Consider shade cloth for heat-sensitive crops if extended heat arrives.
